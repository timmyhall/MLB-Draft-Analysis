ARE MLB SIGNING BONUSES TOO HIGH? AN ANALYSIS OF PLAYER PRODUCTION AND INVESTMENTS IN MLB DRAFT PICKS

Timothy Hall
May 2012
Mathematical Economics
Abstract
Major League Baseball (MLB) teams are often criticized for overspending on amateur draft picks. The casual baseball fan argues that awarding unproven amateur baseball players million dollar signing bonuses are not sound investments. However, previous studies in sports economics have found that teams are willing to sign better players to higher salaries or signing bonuses in order to increase their production and team wins. Similar to the labor market, baseball teams will attempt to create a product (team wins) by employing signing inputs (players who provide skills and services). This paper attempts to quantify or place monetary values on a single total value baseball statistic, Wins Above Replacement (WAR), to determine the Market Value of a baseball playerâ€™s production. WAR represents the additional amount of wins a player contributes to his team over a season. This paper then tests the overall performance and investment of baseball draftees from the 1996 through the 2000 Rule IV Draft. Results of this study reveal that professional baseball teams on average receive a very positive return on their investment in amateur drafts picks.
KEYWORDS: (Major League Baseball, Rule IV Draft, Wins Above Replacement)
